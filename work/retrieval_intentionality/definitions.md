Retrieval intentionality is about whether the memory that surfaces matches what the person is trying to retrieve right now. It is usually operationalized at the task level. Instructions specify whether and what information a participant should seek to retrieve; that is, they specify the intentionality of memory.

Applied at the event level, a recall is voluntary when the item matches the current intention, and involuntary when an unintended item arrives—this can happen even during an intentional recall task. Intrusive memory is a clinical subset of involuntary recall: the item is unintended, unwanted, hard to dismiss, and interfering; vividness is common but not required.

Intrusions in free or serial recall are lab errors (e.g., prior-list, extra-list, repetitions); they are unintended items inside an intentional task, so they are involuntary-in-task, but they are not automatically intrusive unless they also meet unwantedness/control/impact thresholds.

We are interested in clarifying the connection between the additional clinical features of intrusive memory beyond involuntary recall and map them to concepts used in applications of retrieved context theory.



Intrusions = task-general label for unwanted, off-target outputs or thoughts.
In memory experiments: saying a non-studied word on free recall (extra-list intrusion), recalling a word from the wrong list (prior-list intrusion), or repeating an item. They’re scored as errors or commissions.

Intrusive memory / memories = a clinical/cognitive-clinical term for involuntary, recurrent, distressing episodic recollections (often sensory-vivid, “here-and-now”); canonically associated with trauma (e.g., PTSD “flashbacks”). Not all involuntary memories qualify—if they’re neutral/pleasant, they’re usually just called involuntary memories, not intrusive.

Involuntary Memory = a memory that comes to mind without deliberate retrieval attempt. Neutral or positive ones are often called involuntary memories; distressing ones are called intrusive memories.



Involuntary memory = the specific content reaches awareness without intending that content at that moment, regardless of whether you’re in an intentional retrieval task.

Voluntary memory = the content retrieved is the one you were intending to bring to mind right then.

Intentional retrieval = the goal is to bring a specific memory to mind.

Unintentional retrieval = the goal is not to bring that specific memory to mind (may be trying to remember something else, or not trying to remember anything at all).

Volition == intention; the state of having a goal or purpose.

Valence == wanted, neutral, unwanted, distressing, etc.

Control/impact == the degree to which the retrieval is experienced as automatic vs. controlled, or the degree to which it interferes with ongoing activity.

“Intrusive” = unintentional + unwanted/distressing + hard to dismiss (often with sensory “nowness”).
