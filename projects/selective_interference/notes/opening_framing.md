## Current draft

Source: `index.qmd` (consolidated, 2026-03-02). Previous iterations archived below as superseded drafts.

**P1:**
> Traumatic experience foregrounds a trade-off between durability and selectivity in episodic remembering. Memory should retain highly consequential episodes so critical details stay accessible long into the future. At the same time, adaptive retrieval is selective: what comes to mind should normally depend on current cues, goals, and safety. Intrusive memories after trauma suggest that durable encoding can outpace regulatory control. The event returns repeatedly and indiscriminately, despite the demands of the present.

**P2:**
> The present work examines this trade-off in the trauma-film paradigm. Participants watch a distressing film depicting scenes of injury or threat; in the hours and days that follow, memory for the film can surface in two modes of retrieval. Intrusive memories return the event involuntarily: fragments of the film replay without intention, often triggered by incidental cues in daily life. When asked to recall or recognize the same material deliberately, participants often show relatively preserved performance. The same encoded episode can therefore support both uncontrolled and controlled access, and these modes can dissociate.

**P3:**
> A sharper dissociation emerges when a brief visuospatial task follows the film or a later reminder. In several studies, participants who play Tetris report fewer intrusive memories over the following days. Yet voluntary memory for the same material, assessed with standard free recall or recognition tests, often shows little measurable cost. This selective interference effect deepens the puzzle: a post-encoding manipulation appears to alter one mode of retrieval more than the other, even when both draw on the same recent experience.

**P4:**
> A common interpretation attributes this dissociation to separable memory representations. On this view, traumatic experience gives rise to distinct sensory and contextual traces, and a post-encoding task preferentially disrupts the sensory trace while leaving the contextual trace (and the voluntary memory it supports) relatively intact. Here we develop an alternative within a unitary retrieved-context framework and demonstrate it in a formal model. In this account, the degree of contextual overlap between the interference task and the original event governs retrieval competition, while the amount of control available at test governs which memories are expressed. The dissociation between involuntary and voluntary recall emerges from these interacting constraints without requiring separate traces as a modeling prerequisite.

In selintfr, the paradigm (trauma-film) and the diagnostic phenomenon (selective interference) are separable. The paradigm explores P1's tension directly; the selective interference effect is a specific finding within that paradigm that sharpens the puzzle.

## Whole-opening constraints

Evolved from the 17 per-paragraph constraints in `p1_framing.md`. Now applied to the full four-paragraph arc.

### Prose constraints (apply everywhere)

| | Constraint |
|---|---|
| C11 | **One sentence, one job.** No overextension with em-dashes, semicolons, or colons to express multiple ideas. These punctuation marks can still be used if they don't violate the principle. |
| C12 | **Active voice.** Passive and stative copula constructions weaken sentences. Prefer active verbs. Functional register ("preserve," "return," "surface") naturally produces active voice; mechanistic register ("encoded," "gated," "reactivated") invites passives. |
| C15 | **Conciseness.** Target 4–5 sentences per paragraph (P4 may stretch to 5–6 for the dual-theory structure). |
| C16 | **Accessible register.** Avoid gratuitous jargon. Functional language is acceptable when precise. |

### Arc constraints (apply across paragraphs)

| | Constraint |
|---|---|
| A1 | **Linking phrases must connect.** Each paragraph's opening phrase must refer to a concept actually present in the prior paragraph. |
| A2 | **Vocabulary consistency.** Key terms should have one meaning across the opening. If a word is used in two senses, the reader will stumble. |
| A3 | **Forward pull.** Each paragraph should leave the reader wanting what the next paragraph delivers. |
| A4 | **Structurally distinct from repfr.** Both papers may be read by the same reviewers. Different sentence structures and rhetorical moves. |
| A5 | **Hedging calibrated to epistemic status.** Empirical claims hedged where findings are heterogeneous. Theoretical framing uses "in this account" / "suggest" rather than "we show." The opening frames; the paper argues. |

## Evaluation of current draft

### Strengths

1. **Hedging calibrated.** "In several studies," "often," "appears to alter," "relatively preserved" — honest about empirical heterogeneity. (A5)
2. **"Trade-off"** is a strong word for P1's tension. Explicitly signals that gaining one costs the other. (P1-C6)
3. **"Encoded episode"** better than "encoded event" for an episodic memory paper.
4. **"Modes of retrieval"** cleaner than "distinct channels."
5. **"In this account"** (P4) — tonally appropriate. The opening frames, the paper argues. (A5)
6. **"Separable"** (P4) more DRA-accurate than "separate."
7. **"Even when both draw on the same recent experience"** (P3 S4) strengthens the puzzle.
8. **"And demonstrate it in a formal model"** (P4 S3) signals methodology.
9. **All four linking phrases connect cleanly.** P1→P2 "this trade-off," P2→P3 "A sharper dissociation," P3→P4 "this dissociation." (A1)
10. **"Selective interference effect" named in P3.** Anchors the reader in the literature and resolves the selectivity ambiguity with P1. (A2, P3-C1)
11. **P2→P3 link grounded.** "A sharper dissociation" connects directly to P2's "dissociate" without introducing ungrounded vocabulary. (A1)

### Resolved concerns (from prior evaluation)

**1. "Selectivity" ambiguity — resolved.** P1 uses "selectivity" generically; P3 uses "selective interference effect" as a technical term. The compound noun clearly denotes a specific phenomenon, not the same concept as P1's desideratum.

**2. P2→P3 "this balance" — resolved.** Replaced with "A sharper dissociation emerges when..." which links directly to P2's "dissociate." Approach drawn from an earlier draft (block 1, line 13) that handled this transition well.

**4. Selective interference effect unnamed — resolved.** Now named in P3 S4.

### Remaining minor concerns (accepted)

**3. "Durable encoding" (P1 S4) — P1-C13 borderline.**
"Encoding" names a process. More mechanistic than v9's "strong persistence can outpace regulatory control." Still functional-level — any memory researcher accepts the term — but the closest any P1 sentence gets to naming mechanism.

**5. P4 thesis narrower than earlier drafts.**
Earlier P4 v1 dismissed three assumptions (separate traces, reconsolidation windows, modality-specific disruption). Current draft narrows to one ("without requiring separate traces as a modeling prerequisite"). The other two claims survive in the paper's body but aren't previewed in the thesis. Accepted as a scoping choice: the thesis is focused, not comprehensive.

**6. "At the same time" (P1 S3) — soft adversarial connective.**
Softer than v9's "On the other hand." Combined with "trade-off" in S1, the P1-C6 work is sufficient.

<summary>Block 3 (pre-consolidation lines 72–91)</summary>

Traumatic events foreground a trade-off between persistence and selectivity in episodic remembering. Intrusive memories after trauma are often described as involuntary, cue-driven re-experiencing that can recur despite a person's current goals, making them a natural target for accounts that connect basic memory mechanisms to clinical phenomena. This framing motivates a simple question: how can the same episode remain highly accessible while retrieval nonetheless becomes difficult to regulate?

The present work examines this tension in the trauma-film paradigm, an experimental analogue in which participants view distressing film material and subsequently monitor intrusive memories in daily life, commonly using diary methods. A foundational trauma-film study reported that intrusion measures can be weakly related to standard voluntary recall and recognition measures of the same material, consistent with dissociation between involuntary and voluntary expressions of memory. More recent work has refined voluntary-memory tests to better match intrusion content and has still reported robust dissociations under post-encoding interference manipulations.

A visuospatial task performed shortly after film exposure can further shift this balance. In an early demonstration, playing the computer game Tetris after viewing trauma-film material reduced subsequent intrusive "flashback" memories while leaving deliberate memory performance intact on the study's voluntary-memory measures. Related post-encoding visuospatial tasks have likewise been reported to selectively reduce involuntary memories with minimal effects on voluntary recognition. Across the trauma-film literature, a systematic review and meta-analysis finds that cognitive interventions (including visuospatial interference tasks) are associated with fewer intrusions than controls, with timing moderating effects. Interventions delivered after reminder/reactivation procedures at longer delays have also been reported to reduce intrusions, motivating reconsolidation-update interpretations in that line of work.

A common interpretation attributes selective effects on intrusions versus voluntary memory to separable memory representations. In dual-representation-style accounts, traumatic experience yields at least two forms of memory, including a verbally accessible form linked to voluntary report and a situationally accessible form linked to involuntary re-experiencing. Here we develop an alternative within a unitary retrieved-context framework and demonstrate it in a formal model. In this account, contextual overlap between the interference task and the original event governs retrieval competition, while the degree of control available at test governs which memories are expressed. The dissociation between involuntary and voluntary recall emerges from these interacting constraints without requiring separate traces as a modeling prerequisite.
