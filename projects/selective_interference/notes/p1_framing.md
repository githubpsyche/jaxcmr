# Opening Framing — First Paragraph

## Focal problem

The first paragraph must introduce the domain the selective interference effect addresses — not the effect itself. Parallel to repfr, it should: (1) name the capacity under study, (2) identify the challenge to that capacity, and (3) frame a tension that implies the theoretical perspective without stating it.

### Reference: repfr first paragraph

> Episodic memory -- our ability to recall events anchored to a unique time and place -- is especially challenged by repeated experience. A flexible system must navigate two competing demands. It must integrate information across related experiences so partial cues in the present can call back relevant details across the past, yet it must also differentiate those experiences so one moment's details are not confused with those of another. Failure on either front undermines adaptive behavior: without integration, experience stays isolated; without differentiation, experience blurs and we confuse the past.

**Beat structure:**

1. Capacity + challenge: episodic memory is especially challenged by [X]
2. Two competing demands: a flexible system must navigate...
3. Demand A + Demand B: it must [A] yet it must also [B]
4. Stakes of failure: without A, [bad]; without B, [bad]

**The fundamental question (stated cleanly):** "How do we process repeated experience so that each memory is anchored to its own time and place while still identifiable as a repetition?" This works because it names a *constraint the system must satisfy* — a design specification that any successful account must deliver. The tension between integration and differentiation follows directly from it.

### What the selintfr first paragraph must do

If repfr advances a more consistent perspective on the difference between novel and repeated experience, selintfr advances a more consistent perspective on the difference between involuntary and voluntary recall. The first paragraph should introduce the scaffolding for that revised perspective, not merely announce the topic.

## Constraints

1. **Establish a tension.** The paragraph must establish a genuine tension between two functional demands. (The "design-constraint question" form was a useful thinking tool but is not required of the paragraph itself.)
2. **Implies the theoretical perspective.** Should point toward a context-based, single-system account without naming it. (In repfr, "anchored to its own time and place while still identifiable as a repetition" implies differentiated encoding without naming instance theory.)
3. **Clinical and theoretical significance both reachable.** The tension should make the clinical problem (intrusive memories, PTSD) and the theoretical problem (how retrieval modes dissociate) natural downstream consequences.
4. **Not "selective interference."** That comes later. This paragraph introduces the domain that selective interference illuminates.
5. **Same high-level goals as repfr.** The paragraph should introduce the domain, establish a tension, and convey stakes — but is not bound to repfr's specific beat structure or sentence count.
6. **The tension must be genuine.** The two demands must pull against each other, not merely coexist. In repfr, integration and differentiation compete for the same representational resources. Here, the two demands must similarly be in friction.
7. **Dysfunctional framing.** Unlike repfr, which describes a design problem the system *solves* (both sides are functional achievements), selintfr concerns a design problem the system *fails to solve* for traumatic experience. The template must accommodate this: both sides of the tension are real demands, but the phenomenon of interest is what happens when one overwhelms the other.
8. **Structurally distinct from repfr.** Different sentence structures and rhetorical moves. Cannot read as a reskin of the repfr opening — both papers may be read by the same reviewers.
9. **Signals trauma memory early.** The reader should recognize the domain (trauma and memory) early enough that the general framing doesn't feel disconnected from the paper's subject. Don't bury it, but building to it is acceptable.
10. **No implication that trauma is beneficial.** No framing that implies trauma is instructive or that traumatic memories serve the person who has them. "Hard-won lessons" and similar language is out.
11. **One sentence, one job.** No overextension with em-dashes, semicolons, or colons to express multiple ideas or belabour the same point. These can still be used if they don't violate this principle.
12. **Active voice.** Passive and stative copula constructions weaken sentences. Prefer active verbs.
13. **Name the problem, not the mechanism.** The opening paragraph frames the tension in terms any memory researcher would accept, regardless of theoretical commitments. The specific account (context-based encoding, competitive retrieval, encoding strength) belongs downstream. The repfr first paragraph doesn't assert *how* the system solves the integration/differentiation problem — it just names the problem.
14. **Traumatic memory ≠ pathological memory.** "Traumatic memory" means memory for a traumatic event, which is usually normal. Most people who experience trauma form memories that function fine — unpleasant but not intrusive, fading appropriately, available when relevant. The pathological case (intrusive, persistent, uncontrollable retrieval) is the minority outcome. The paragraph must not treat all memory for trauma as dysfunctional. The failure mode is about *retrieval* escaping normal governance, not about the memory itself being broken.
15. **Conciseness.** Target 4–5 sentences. An opening paragraph that runs longer loses punch.
16. **Accessible register.** This is a theoretical/modeling paper. Accessible register still matters for broad impact, but the constraint is less stringent than for an empirical journal. Avoid gratuitous jargon ("retrieval governance") or overly biological register ("organism"), but functional language is acceptable when precise.
17. **Sets up paragraph two.** The paragraph should leave the reader wanting to know *why* the system sometimes fails and *what can be done about it*. It should function as a setup, not a self-contained statement.

## The tension

### Rejected: responsiveness vs. controllability

The system must be responsive enough that relevant memories surface without deliberate effort yet structured enough that retrieval can be directed toward specific targets. Problem: this casts involuntary retrieval (broad reachability by incidental context) as a functional desideratum on equal footing with deliberate retrieval. But involuntary retrieval isn't a goal the system optimizes for — it's a *consequence* of the architecture. Framing it as a desideratum mischaracterizes the relationship.

### Rejected: retrieval selectivity over non-selective associations

Context-driven retrieval is inherently competitive: everything encoded in similar context competes. The functional problem is achieving targeted access over this non-selective structure. The involuntary/voluntary distinction falls out of whether control is applied. Problem: this is the right mechanistic insight but it names the *answer* (retrieval control over competitive associations) rather than the *functional problem* the system faces. Too architectural to serve as an opening framing.

### Current: preservation vs. relevance

The system must *preserve* high-impact events so critical experiences remain accessible long after they occur, yet must also keep retrieval *tuned to present needs* so what comes to mind serves current circumstances and goals. The same encoding strength that achieves preservation undermines relevance-tuning. Intrusive memories are what happens when preservation overwhelms relevance: the past intrudes on the present.

**Why this works:**
- Both sides are genuine functional demands (the system MUST preserve important experience; the system MUST tune retrieval to the present)
- They pull against each other: stronger encoding preserves better but makes the memory harder to keep from intruding
- Implies the context-based architecture without naming it: "present circumstances" → context state; "accessible long after" → strong contextual binding that resists drift
- Clinical significance is immediate: intrusive memories = preservation overwhelming relevance
- Theoretical significance follows: a single-system account where the same mechanism (contextual binding) serves both preservation and relevance, and the balance between them determines whether retrieval is adaptive or pathological

## Rhetorical shape

### Rejected: "two competing demands" (repfr shape)

The repfr paragraph uses a symmetric structure: demand A vs. demand B, failure on either side. This works for repfr because both sides are functional achievements in tension. For selintfr, this shape has three problems: (1) too recognizably similar to repfr (C8), (2) the symmetric framing doesn't capture the dysfunctional direction of the paper (C7), and (3) it delays signaling trauma (C9).

### Partially rejected: adaptive bias → pathological overshoot

The system encodes emotionally intense events with particular strength — a general bias that is adaptive because remembering what was dangerous or consequential serves survival. Trauma is where this bias becomes pathological: the encoding was so strong that the memory persists and intrudes regardless of whether present circumstances call for it.

**What worked:** Different rhetorical shape from repfr (C8). Signals trauma early (C9). Motivates preservation without implying trauma is beneficial (C10).

**What didn't work:** The shape depends on asserting a specific mechanism (encoding strength → persistence → intrusion) as the explanation. This violates C13 — the opening paragraph should name the problem, not the mechanism. Also implies the bias always overshoots for trauma, when the pathological case is the exception. The adaptive-bias idea may still be usable downstream (e.g., paragraph two or three), but it's too committed for paragraph one.

### Current: preservation vs. relevance (problem-level)

The tension between preservation and relevance can be stated at the problem level without asserting a mechanism. Emotionally intense events demand privileged access in memory. Yet retrieval must also serve the present. Traumatic memory is where this tension breaks down — the event persists and intrudes regardless of whether current circumstances call for it.

**Why this works:**
- Both sides are genuine functional demands any memory researcher would accept (C13)
- They pull against each other (C6)
- Implies the context-based architecture without naming it: "present circumstances" → context state; "privileged access" → strong encoding that resists drift (C2)
- Clinical significance is immediate: intrusive memories = preservation overwhelming relevance (C3)
- Doesn't assert *why* the tension breaks down for trauma — that's the paper's job (C13)

## Drafts of the fundamental question

### v1

> How does a single memory system support both involuntary and voluntary access to the same event — and what makes these modes differentially vulnerable to disruption?

**Problem:** Reads as a research question, not a design constraint. "What makes these modes differentially vulnerable" asks what we want to explain rather than specifying what the system must accomplish. Doesn't identify the point of friction.

### v2

> How does the system make a memory broadly reachable by incidental context yet precisely targetable by deliberate retrieval?

**Problem:** Closer to a constraint — names two demands (broad reachability, precise targetability) in tension. But casts broad reachability by incidental context as a functional desideratum, when it's really a consequence of the architecture, not a goal. Also: empirical constraint rather than functional target.

### v3

> How does the system keep high-impact experiences readily accessible while still tuning retrieval to what the present moment demands?

**Problem:** Both sides are positive demands. But "readily accessible" is vague — doesn't convey the *strength* of traumatic encoding or why it resists normal forgetting.

### v4 — dysfunctional reframe

> How do we process traumatic experience so that its memory becomes both unforgettable and uncontrollable?

**Advance:** Accepts the dysfunctional framing. Names two properties of traumatic memory that coexist — one that looks like the system succeeding (unforgettable = preservation of important experience) and one that reveals the cost (uncontrollable = intrusion). Both come from the same encoding. Compact.

**Problem:** Language is placeholder. "Unforgettable" and "uncontrollable" are evocative but imprecise — need to find the right concrete terms.

**C6 — the deepest problem (C6/C13 structural tension):**

In repfr, integration and differentiation trade off *definitionally* — merging representations loses distinctiveness, and vice versa. The tension is self-evident from the demands themselves. You don't need a mechanistic story to see why they conflict.

Here, preservation and present-sensitivity are NOT definitionally in tension. A memory could in principle be strongly preserved AND selectively retrieved. The conflict only emerges from *mechanism*: strong encoding creates traces that compete broadly for retrieval regardless of contextual relevance. But C13 forbids asserting that mechanism.

So the paragraph can only show the tension through its failure (S3–S4: the balance breaks down, the event intrudes) rather than derive it from the demands. The reader must accept S1's "while" as signaling genuine friction, then see the tension confirmed by the failure. But without understanding WHY the demands conflict, the reader may think "sometimes intrusive memories happen" rather than "there's a fundamental trade-off at play."

This is a structural tension between C6 and C13 that may be inherent to the domain. Options: (a) relax C6 to accept "tension demonstrated through failure" as sufficient, (b) relax C13 enough to hint at WHY the demands conflict, or (c) find a way to frame preservation and present-sensitivity as more obviously in tension without asserting mechanism.

**C11 (S2 em-dash):** "remain governed by this balance — they persist, but resurface in step with current circumstances and goals." The em-dash appends elaboration of what "governed" means. Same pattern rejected in v2/v4. Mitigating: the elaboration is necessary because "governed by this balance" is too abstract without it. Alternative: split into two sentences ("...remain governed by this balance. They persist, but resurface in step with current circumstances and goals."). Trade-off: splitting costs a sentence (C15) and weakens the flow.

**C12 (S2 + S5):** S2 "remain governed by" — stative/passive. S5 "stays governed" — stative/passive; "is a question" — copula. "Governed" appears three times across the paragraph (S2, S5 twice), compounding the problem.

**Key finding — C6 is resolved, and the C6/C13 dilemma is not inherent to the domain:**

v6's deepest problem was C6: the tension between preservation and present-sensitivity isn't self-evident from the demands. v6 used "while" in a single clause, which the reader might read as two compatible goals rather than competing demands. The C6/C13 tension seemed possibly inherent to the domain.

But the repfr structure resolves C6 through *rhetoric*, not mechanism:
- "**Two competing demands**" explicitly frames them as adversarial
- "**Yet**" signals friction between the parallel clauses
- "**When the balance tips toward preservation**" implies a seesaw — you can't maximize both

None of this asserts mechanism (C13 safe). The repfr structure earns the tension through adversarial framing and the balance/tipping metaphor. When v6 abandoned the repfr structure for C8, it lost these rhetorical features, and C6 became a problem.

**Implication:** C8 was masking C6's real source. The C6/C13 dilemma is not inherent to this domain — it's an artifact of stripping out the adversarial rhetoric. A paragraph that keeps "competing," "yet," and the balance metaphor can make the tension feel genuine without asserting mechanism. The question for v7: can it borrow these specific rhetorical features while still satisfying C8?

The essential features for C6 are: (a) explicit adversarial framing of the demands, (b) a connective that signals friction ("yet" rather than "while"), (c) a balance/tipping metaphor. These features are not unique to the repfr sentence structure — they could live in a structurally different paragraph.

### v7 — durability vs. selectivity (user draft)
