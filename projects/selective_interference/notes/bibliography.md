# Annotated Bibliography

Key references for the selective interference paper, organized by topic. Each entry notes what the paper contributes to the argument. Citation keys match `references.bib`.

## Contextual binding and retrieved-context theory

### @yonelinas2019contextual
Yonelinas, A. P., Ranganath, C., Ekstrom, A. D., & Wiltgen, B. J. (2019). A contextual binding theory of episodic memory: Systems consolidation reconsidered. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 20*(6), 364–375.

Provides the broader neurocognitive framework for the paper's account. Argues that episodic memory works by binding events to context, and that forgetting arises from contextual interference rather than time-dependent consolidation. Grounds the computational mechanisms of CMR in a well-known theoretical framework accessible to clinical and neuroscience audiences. Subsection 1 ¶1 opens with this.

### @howard2002distributed
Howard, M. W., & Kahana, M. J. (2002). A distributed representation of temporal context. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 46*(3), 269–299.

Foundational paper for retrieved-context theory. Introduces the Temporal Context Model (TCM): items are bound to a continuously evolving temporal context, and retrieving an item reinstates its associated context. The primary source for the context-to-item and item-to-context distinction that drives the selective interference account.

### @polyn2009context
Polyn, S. M., Norman, K. A., & Kahana, M. J. (2009). A context maintenance and retrieval model of organizational processes in free recall. *Psychological Review, 116*(1), 129–156.

Introduces CMR (Context Maintenance and Retrieval), which extends TCM with source-context dimensions and retrieval control mechanisms. The model architecture used in the present paper. Adds the start-of-context reinstatement and competition-sharpening mechanisms that explain graded protection of deliberate recall.

### @sederberg2008context
Sederberg, P. B., Howard, M. W., & Kahana, M. J. (2008). A context-based theory of recency and contiguity in free recall. *Psychological Review, 115*(4), 893–912.

Shows that temporal contiguity effects persist across long filled delays, supporting the claim that retrieved context (not simple recency) drives memory transitions. Key evidence that context reinstatement is the engine of episodic memory search.

### @kahana1996associative
Kahana, M. J. (1996). Associative retrieval processes in free recall. *Memory & Cognition, 24*(1), 103–109.

Establishes the temporal contiguity effect (lag-CRP): successive recalls tend to come from nearby study positions with a forward bias. The primary empirical regularity that context-to-item retrieval explains. Cited in the wins catalog.

### @kahana2020computational
Kahana, M. J. (2020). Computational models of memory search. *Annual Review of Psychology, 71*, 107–138.

Comprehensive review of retrieved-context theory and its applications. Useful for readers unfamiliar with the framework. Provides context for the two retrieval pathways and their behavioral signatures.

### @talmi2019retrieved
Talmi, D., Lohnas, L. J., & Daw, N. D. (2019). A retrieved context model of the emotional modulation of memory. *Psychological Review, 126*(4), 455–485.

Introduces eCMR, which adds emotional arousal and valence as context dimensions. High-arousal events are encoded more strongly and bound to arousal context states. Directly relevant: provides the mechanism by which traumatic (high-arousal) film material is encoded with enhanced strength and emotional context.

### @howard2008persistence
Howard, M. W., Youker, T. E., & Venkatadass, V. S. (2008). The persistence of memory: Contiguity effects across hundreds of seconds. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 15*(1), 58–63.

Demonstrates that temporal contiguity persists across very long delays. Part of the wins catalog.

### @zaromb2006temporal
Zaromb, F. M., Howard, M. W., Dolan, E. D., Sirotin, Y. B., Tully, M., Wingfield, A., & Kahana, M. J. (2006). Temporal associations and prior-list intrusions in free recall. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition, 32*(4), 792–804.

Shows graded prior-list intrusions: memories from recent but irrelevant episodes intrude during recall with a frequency that decays over intervening time. Directly relevant to the intrusion mechanism in the trauma-film paradigm.

### @lohnas2015expanding
Lohnas, L. J., Polyn, S. M., & Kahana, M. J. (2015). Expanding the scope of memory search: Modeling intralist and interlist effects in free recall. *Psychological Review, 122*(2), 337–363.

Models both within-list and between-list effects in free recall, including prior-list intrusions. Demonstrates that retrieved-context models can capture interference from recent prior experiences.

### @mensink1989model
Mensink, G.-J. M., & Raaijmakers, J. G. W. (1989). A model for contextual fluctuation. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 33*(2), 172–186.

Early context-fluctuation model. Context drifts independently of study events. Cited as the baseline view that retrieved-context theory departs from (in repfr) and as evidence that contextual interference explains forgetting (in the present paper, via Yonelinas 2019).

### @estes1955statistical
Estes, W. K. (1955). Statistical theory of spontaneous recovery and regression. *Psychological Review, 62*(3), 145–154.

Early stimulus-sampling theory treating context as an independent random process. Part of the theoretical lineage that contextual binding theory builds on.

## Trauma-film paradigm

### @holmes2004
Holmes, E. A., Brewin, C. R., & Hennessy, R. G. (2004). Trauma films, information processing, and intrusive memory development. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 133*(1), 3–22.

Foundational trauma-film study. Establishes that film-induced intrusions can be measured via diary methods and that intrusion frequency can dissociate from voluntary memory measures. P2 cites this for the initial dissociation finding.

### @holmes2008
Holmes, E. A., & Bourne, C. (2008). Inducing and modulating intrusive emotional memories: A review of the trauma film paradigm. *Acta Psychologica, 127*(3), 553–566.

Review of the trauma-film paradigm as an experimental analogue for intrusive memories. Establishes the methodological framework used across the selective interference literature.

### @holmes2009
Holmes, E. A., James, E. L., Coode-Bate, T., & Deeprose, C. (2009). Can playing the computer game "Tetris" reduce the build-up of flashbacks for trauma? A proposal from cognitive science. *PLoS ONE, 4*(1), e4153.

Widely cited demonstration that playing Tetris after viewing traumatic film material reduces subsequent intrusive memories while leaving voluntary memory intact. The original selective interference effect in the trauma-film paradigm.

### @holmes2010
Holmes, E. A., James, E. L., Kilford, E. J., & Deeprose, C. (2010). Key steps in developing a cognitive vaccine against traumatic flashbacks: Visuospatial Tetris versus verbal Pub Quiz. *PLoS ONE, 5*(11), e13706.

Compares visuospatial (Tetris) vs verbal (Pub Quiz) interference tasks. The visuospatial task selectively reduces intrusions; the verbal task does not. DRA interprets this as modality-specific disruption. The present account interprets this as a difference in encoding quality and engagement, not modality per se.

### @james2015
James, E. L., Bonsall, M. B., Hoppitt, L., Tunbridge, E. M., Geddes, J. R., Milton, A. L., & Holmes, E. A. (2015). Computer game play reduces intrusive memories of experimental trauma via reconsolidation-update mechanisms. *Psychological Science, 26*(8), 1201–1215.

Demonstrates selective interference at delay: a reminder + Tetris reduces intrusions while sparing voluntary memory. Introduces reconsolidation framing. The present paper offers an alternative to the reconsolidation interpretation via context reinstatement.

### @james2016
James, E. L., Lau-Zhu, A., Clark, I. A., Visser, R. M., Hagenaars, M. A., & Holmes, E. A. (2016). The trauma film paradigm as an experimental psychopathology model of psychological trauma: Intrusive memories and beyond. *Clinical Psychology Review, 47*, 106–142.

Comprehensive review of the trauma-film paradigm. Establishes the methodological and theoretical landscape for the present work.

### @lauzhu2019
Lau-Zhu, A., Holmes, E. A., Butterfield, S., & Holmes, J. (2019). Selective association between Tetris game play and visuospatial working memory: A preliminary investigation. *Applied Cognitive Psychology, 33*(3), 438–445.

### @deeprose2012
Deeprose, C., Zhang, S., Dejong, H., Sherwood Sherborne, R., & Holmes, E. A. (2012). Can playing the computer game "Tetris" reduce intrusive memories for trauma? *Behaviour Research and Therapy, 50*, 507–514.

Replication of Tetris reducing intrusions post-film. Part of the evidence base for P3.

### @kessler2020
Kessler, H., et al. (2020). *[Verify full reference.]* Visuospatial interference reduces intrusions after trauma-film exposure. Part of the evidence base for the selective interference effect.

### @asselbergs2023
Asselbergs, J., et al. (2023). *[Verify full reference.]* Shows voluntary memory is spared even under matched retrieval conditions. Cited in P3 for the claim that the dissociation persists under careful methodological controls.

## Clinical translation

### @iyadurai2018
Iyadurai, L., et al. (2018). *[Verify full reference.]* Demonstrates that visuospatial interference (Tetris) after directed recollection of a real-life traumatic memory reduces subsequent intrusions in motor vehicle accident survivors. Key evidence that the selective interference effect translates from laboratory to clinical populations.

### @iyadurai2019
Iyadurai, L., Visser, R. M., Lau-Zhu, A., Porcheret, K., Horsch, A., Holmes, E. A., & James, E. L. (2019). Intrusive memories of trauma: A target for research bridging cognitive science and its clinical application. *Clinical Psychology Review, 69*, 67–82.

Review bridging cognitive science and clinical application of intrusive memory research. Frames intrusive memories as a target for intervention.

### @horsch2017
Horsch, A., et al. (2017). *[Verify full reference.]* Demonstrates that visuospatial interference after directed recollection reduces intrusions following traumatic childbirth (emergency caesarean section). Clinical evidence for P3 S4.

### @kanstrup2021single
Kanstrup, M., Kontio, E., Geranmayeh, A., Olofsdotter Lauri, K., Moulds, M. L., & Holmes, E. A. (2021). A single case series using visuospatial task interference to reduce the number of visual intrusive memories of trauma with refugees. *Clinical Psychology & Psychotherapy, 28*(4), 1–11.

Demonstrates visuospatial interference with refugees reporting war-related trauma. Clinical evidence for P3 S4. Extends the paradigm beyond laboratory settings to real-world traumatic experiences.

## Dual-representation account

### @brewin1996
Brewin, C. R., Dalgleish, T., & Joseph, S. (1996). A dual representation theory of posttraumatic stress disorder. *Psychological Review, 103*(4), 670–686.

Original dual-representation theory. Proposes separate sensory (S-rep) and contextual (C-rep) memory systems. S-reps support intrusive re-experiencing; C-reps support deliberate recall. The paper under scrutiny in P4.

### @brewin2010
Brewin, C. R., Gregory, J. D., Lipton, M., & Burgess, N. (2010). Intrusive images in psychological disorders: Characteristics, neural mechanisms, and treatment implications. *Psychological Review, 117*(1), 210–232.

Updated dual-representation account incorporating neural mechanisms. Cited in P4 for the functional distinction between sensory and contextual traces.

### @brewin2014
Brewin, C. R. (2014). Episodic memory, perceptual memory, and their interaction: Foundations for a theory of posttraumatic stress disorder. *Psychological Bulletin, 140*(1), 69–97.

Further development of the dual-representation account. Cited in P4 for the claim that visuospatial interference selectively disrupts the sensory trace.

## Reconsolidation

### @kindt2009beyond
Kindt, M., Soeter, M., & Vervliet, B. (2009). Beyond extinction: Erasing human fear responses and preventing the return of fear. *Nature Neuroscience, 12*(3), 256–258.

Landmark reconsolidation study. Demonstrates that reactivation of a consolidated fear memory followed by a pharmacological intervention can prevent its return. Cited in P4 for the reconsolidation framing that the present account challenges.
