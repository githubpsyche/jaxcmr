## P1

Full derivation history (v1–v9) in `p1_framing.md`. Key milestones summarized here.

### v1 — beat-for-beat repfr parallel (rejected)

> Episodic memory — our ability to recall events anchored to a unique time and place — is especially challenged by traumatic experience. A flexible system must navigate two competing demands. It must preserve high-impact events so that critical experiences remain accessible long after they occur, yet it must also keep retrieval tuned to present needs so that what comes to mind serves current circumstances and goals. Failure on either front undermines adaptive behavior: without preservation, hard-won lessons are lost; without relevance, the past overwhelms the present.

Too structurally similar to repfr (C8). "Hard-won lessons" implies trauma is instructive (C10). Trauma buried until S4 (C9).

### v5 — individual differences (rejected)

> Memory normally preserves what matters while staying tuned to the present. Even after a traumatic event, most people form memories that fade and resurface in step with current circumstances. But for some, the memory of the event escapes this balance. It returns repeatedly and without invitation, regardless of what the present demands. Understanding why the same event can produce a memory that tracks present circumstances in one person and overwhelms them in another is a central challenge for theories of episodic memory.

Wrong tension: centers individual differences ("most people vs. some people"), not system-level functional demands.

### v8 — circularity resolved, functional register (rejected)

> Emotionally intense events demand privileged access in memory — they must persist so that what was threatening or traumatic remains accessible months or years later. Yet retrieval must also serve the present, keeping what comes to mind tuned to current cues, goals, and safety. Intrusive memories after trauma illustrate what happens when preservation overwhelms this balance. The event returns repeatedly across situations where it does not help, regardless of what the present demands.

C6/C12/C14 all resolved. S1 em-dash circular (restates rather than motivates preservation). 4 sentences — maximally concise but lost S1 motivation.

### v9 — "on the one hand / on the other hand" (superseded 2026-03-03)

> Traumatic events highlight a tension between persistence and regulation in episodic remembering. On the one hand, memory should retain highly consequential episodes so critical details stay accessible long into the future. On the other hand, adaptive retrieval is targeted: what comes to mind should depend on current cues, goals, and safety. Intrusive memories after trauma suggest that strong persistence can outpace regulatory control. The event returns repeatedly and indiscriminately, regardless of present demands.

All major constraints satisfied. "On the one hand / on the other hand" structurally distinct from repfr (C8). Superseded by current draft which changed: "highlight" → "foregrounds," "persistence and regulation" → "durability and selectivity," "on the one hand/other hand" → "memory should.../at the same time," "targeted" → "selective," "strong persistence" → "durable encoding," "regardless of present demands" → "despite the demands of the present."

## P2

### Block 3 — experimental analogue framing (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> The present work examines this tension in the trauma-film paradigm, an experimental analogue in which participants view distressing film material and subsequently monitor intrusive memories in daily life, commonly using diary methods. A foundational trauma-film study reported that intrusion measures can be weakly related to standard voluntary recall and recognition measures of the same material, consistent with dissociation between involuntary and voluntary expressions of memory. More recent work has refined voluntary-memory tests to better match intrusion content and has still reported robust dissociations under post-encoding interference manipulations.

Procedural rather than conceptual. Too much citation-speak ("a foundational trauma-film study reported..."). Doesn't name the two retrieval modes as a conceptual dissociation.

### Block 1 — method-heavy (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> The present work examines this trade-off in the trauma-film paradigm. Participants view a distressing film and then monitor intrusive memories over subsequent days, most often using diary methods. In parallel, voluntary memory for the same material is usually assessed using recognition or free recall tests. Early trauma-film work reported that intrusion frequency was largely unrelated to voluntary recall and recognition measures of the same material, consistent with divergence between involuntary and voluntary outcomes within the same participants. More recent work has reinforced this conclusion with designs that measure intrusions and voluntary memory side by side under matched conditions.

5 sentences but reads as methods description. "Early trauma-film work reported" → citation-speak. Doesn't arrive at the conceptual point (same episode, two modes) until the reader has waded through procedural detail.

### Block 2 — phenomenological (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> The present work examines this trade-off in the trauma-film paradigm. Participants watch a distressing film depicting scenes of injury or threat. In the hours and days that follow, they commonly report spontaneous intrusive memories of film content in daily life, often measured with diary methods. When asked to recall or recognize the same material deliberately, voluntary memory can be relatively preserved, and its relationship to intrusion frequency can be weak, such that the two measures dissociate. The same encoded episode can therefore support both uncontrolled and controlled access, and these modes can come apart empirically.

Close to current. Minor differences: S3 "they commonly report spontaneous intrusive memories of film content in daily life, often measured with diary methods" → current drops the phenomenological detail and diary-methods reference. Current S3 is more concise: "Intrusive memories commonly emerge over the following days in diary measures."

### Notes 4-paragraph version — two modes of retrieval (superseded 2026-03-03)

> The present work examines this trade-off in the trauma-film paradigm. Participants watch a distressing film depicting scenes of injury or threat; in the hours and days that follow, memory for the film can surface in two modes of retrieval. Intrusive memories return the event involuntarily: fragments of the film replay without intention, often triggered by incidental cues in daily life. When asked to recall or recognize the same material deliberately, participants often show relatively preserved performance. The same encoded episode can therefore support both uncontrolled and controlled access, and these modes can dissociate.

Cleaner conceptual framing ("two modes of retrieval") but lacking empirical grounding. S2 semicolon joins paradigm + modes in one sentence (C11 borderline). S3 colon elaboration. Current version favors concreteness (diary measures, citation-able claims) over conceptual elegance.

## P3

### Block 1 — minimal (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> A sharper dissociation emerges when a brief visuospatial interference task follows the film or a later reminder. Across studies, the task reduces diary-recorded intrusions relative to controls, without a measurable cost to deliberate memory in the same experiments.

Only 2 sentences. Doesn't name the effect (P3-C1). Doesn't cover delayed clinical interventions (P3-C2 partial). No sharpening of the puzzle (P3-C3).

### Block 3 — citation-heavy (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> A visuospatial task performed shortly after film exposure can further shift this balance. In an early demonstration, playing the computer game Tetris after viewing trauma-film material reduced subsequent intrusive "flashback" memories while leaving deliberate memory performance intact on the study's voluntary-memory measures. Related post-encoding visuospatial tasks have likewise been reported to selectively reduce involuntary memories with minimal effects on voluntary recognition. Across the trauma-film literature, a systematic review and meta-analysis finds that cognitive interventions (including visuospatial interference tasks) are associated with fewer intrusions than controls, with timing moderating effects. Interventions delivered after reminder/reactivation procedures at longer delays have also been reported to reduce intrusions, motivating reconsolidation-update interpretations in that line of work.

5 sentences but reads as lit review. Too much citation-speak. Doesn't name the selective interference effect. Final sentence introduces reconsolidation (P4's territory).

### Block 2 — meta-analytic framing (pre-consolidation, 2026-03-02)

> A visuospatial task performed shortly after the film can shift this balance. In a widely cited demonstration, participants who played Tetris after viewing traumatic film material reported fewer later intrusive "flashback" memories, while deliberate memory for the film was reported as intact on the study's voluntary-memory measures. Across studies, meta-analytic work similarly supports the claim that some post-film cognitive interventions, including visuospatial interference tasks, reduce intrusion frequency in the trauma-film paradigm, with effects depending on factors such as timing. This selectivity sharpens the puzzle: a post-encoding manipulation can alter involuntary retrieval more than voluntary performance on standard tests, even when both concern the same recent experience.

"Sharpens the puzzle" presupposes a puzzle (C17 violation). "This selectivity" in S4 works as P4 link but names it too generically. Missing clinical translation (no delayed real-life interventions).

### Notes 4-paragraph version (superseded 2026-03-03)

> A sharper dissociation emerges when a brief visuospatial task follows the film or a later reminder. In several studies, participants who play Tetris report fewer intrusive memories over the following days. Yet voluntary memory for the same material, assessed with standard free recall or recognition tests, often shows little measurable cost. This selective interference effect deepens the puzzle: a post-encoding manipulation appears to alter one mode of retrieval more than the other, even when both draw on the same recent experience.

Names the effect (P3-C1 ✓). But: "deepens the puzzle" still uses unearned "puzzle" framing (C17). No clinical translation. No post-reminder evidence. "Sharpens the puzzle" → "deepens the puzzle" attempted to fix C17 but same underlying problem.

## P4

### Combined P4 — all pre-split versions (superseded 2026-03-03)

All pre-split drafts had a combined P4 mixing DRA (S1–S2) and RCT thesis (S3–S5). The three pre-consolidation blocks, the notes version, and the index.qmd version all shared this structure with minor wording differences.

Representative (index.qmd 4-paragraph version):

> A prominent interpretation attributes this selectivity to separable memory representations. Under this dual representation account, traumatic experience gives rise to distinct sensory and contextual traces, and a post-encoding task preferentially disrupts the sensory trace while leaving the contextual trace (and the voluntary memory it supports) relatively intact. Here we develop an alternative within a unitary retrieved-context framework and demonstrate it in a formal model. In this account, the degree of contextual overlap between the interference task and the original event governs retrieval competition, while the amount of control available at test governs which memories are expressed. The dissociation between involuntary and voluntary recall emerges from these interacting constraints without requiring separate traces as a modeling prerequisite.

Split into P4+P5. See decisions.md #3 for the five problems motivating the split. Key issues: DRA got only 2 sentences; no reconsolidation coverage; thesis named only one of three DRA assumptions challenged.

## P5

P5 did not exist before the P4/P5 split (2026-03-03). Its content was extracted from the RCT portion (S3–S5) of the combined P4.

### v1 — initial 5-sentence version (superseded 2026-03-03)

> Here we develop an alternative within a unitary retrieved-context framework and demonstrate it through simulations of a formal model. The framework holds that items are bound to a continuously evolving temporal context that later serves as the primary retrieval cue. In this account, the original event and a later interference task deposit traces in this shared context, and the degree of overlap between their contexts governs retrieval competition. Retrieval control mechanisms available during deliberate recall — reinstating the event's context and sharpening competition among candidates — can partially restore the target's advantage over competitors. The dissociation between involuntary and voluntary recall emerges from these interacting constraints without requiring separate traces, reconsolidation windows, or modality-specific disruption as modeling prerequisites.

5 sentences. All constraints P5-C1 through P5-C4 satisfied. Superseded: P5-C5 (closing motivation) not yet defined. S4 used em-dash elaboration; "partially restore" subsequently changed to "preserve." Missing motivation sentence — ends on parsimony without saying why the alternative matters.
