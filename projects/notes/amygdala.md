# What does the amygdala do to emotional memory?

> the amygdala may be an essential link in a subcortical circuit mediating the formation and permanent (or “indelible”) storage of “conditioned fear” [[CahillMcGaugh1999]].

> "It is suggested that the effect of amygdalectomy is to make it difficult for animals to identify reinforcing stimuli" [[weiskrantz1956behavioral]].

> “…attaching `fear’ to the previously neutral stimulus and remembering it is what the amygdala does” [[stevens1998million]].

It looks like the foundational paper to cite is [[weiskrantz1956behavioral]], which is cited in other reviews like [[CahillMcGaugh1999]].

Even by late 1990s, the amygdala is highlighted as fundamentally about emotional memory, the association of fear with neutral stimuli.

[[CahillMcGaugh1998]] raises the hypotheses that "emotional events are better remembered because they are novel, focus attention, or are often rehearsed" as the basis of some psychological accounts, but says these "have failed fully to account for the experimental findings." The cited evidence for this is in a book chapter I can't reach but it's a striking claim. 

I have to be careful though to distinguish claims about what the amygdala does to memories vs what emotionality in an event does to memories.
