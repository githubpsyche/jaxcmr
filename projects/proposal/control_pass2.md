SPECIFIC AIMS
The goal of this project is to develop and test a computational model of retrieval intentionality that provides mechanistic explanations for human performance and electrophysiological brain activity in free‑recall tasks using cognitive machinery from retrieved‑context models of episodic memory (e.g., temporal context; Howard & Kahana, 2002; Polyn et al., 2009) augmented with explicit control (gating) and stopping policies. This model challenges an inflential proposal in the intrusive‑memory literature: that involuntary recall reflects a separate, spontaneous route untethered from ongoing cognitive context. In our framework, a shared cue‑driven generator produces candidate memories as soon as context is updated; voluntary versus involuntary recall reflects the state of control and stopping mechanisms, with failures of control yielding intrusions. This theoretical unification links emotion/reward effects, strategic performance, and intrusive recollection within one architecture.

Our approach is to analyze existing behavioral and EEG datasets from human free‑recall tasks with emotional and reward manipulations to iteratively refine a computational model (as shown in Fig. 1) of performance and brain signals. The model at the heart of our proposal is a retrieved‑context account in which a gradually changing temporal context is encoded into episodic traces and drives cue‑based retrieval. We extend this framework with an instruction‑dependent gate and a normative stopping policy to explain voluntary recall, and with EEG‑informed modulators of control/stop to explain trial‑wise variability and involuntary output. The planned analyses adjudicate competing model variants differing in (i) whether involuntary recall requires a separate route, (ii) the architecture of gating/monitoring versus stopping, and (iii) the role of item‑independent temporal drift in cross‑list intrusions. We will deploy this approach to accomplish the following specific aims:

Aim 1: Test competing accounts of the voluntary/involuntary distinction in a retrieved‑context framework.
In three analyses across existing multi‑list free‑recall datasets, we (A1) contrast a no‑distinction model (shared generator only), a shared‑generator‑plus‑control model (online gate + stop), and a separate‑route model; (A2) evaluate intrusion rates, latency distributions, and termination times under neutral versus suppression instructions; and (A3) examine emotion‑matched versus neutral intrusions to test whether control failures, not a distinct generator, explain “spontaneous” outputs. Out‑of‑sample likelihood and model‑recovery tests adjudicate among architectures.

Aim 2: Model voluntary recall as consistency with retrieval goals that inform a task policy that adapts with instructions and practice.
We modify the benchmark analyses from Aim 1 to introduce a normative utility‑based ordering and dynamic stopping policy (reward‑weighted free recall; suppression instructions). In two analyses, we (B1) derive the optimal recall/stop policy given rewards, time costs, and intrusion penalties and test policy‑gap predictions for observed order and stop times; and (B2) model practice‑driven changes in gate/stop parameters across lists/sessions (multi‑trial learning), linking reduced policy gaps to fewer intrusions and faster termination.

Aim 3: Build a neurocognitive model linking EEG to voluntary and involuntary recall.
Using existing EEG recordings aligned to encoding and retrieval, we (C1) compute retrieval‑locked and pre‑response features indexing control, reinstatement, and evidence accumulation; (C2) use these signals as priors/modulators on the gate and stop threshold (and optional temporal drift) within the same model; and (C3) test whether EEG‑informed parameters improve prediction of trial‑wise intrusions and termination on held‑out recalls, with shuffled‑feature controls to assess specificity.
