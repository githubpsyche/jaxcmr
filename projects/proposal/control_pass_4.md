We Remember for Reasons: Control Mechanisms for Goal-Directed Memory Search

The broad objective of memory is to relate past experience to ongoing needs. However, external cues are often too sparse or misleading to do that unaided. Humans solve this by launching an internal memory search, constructing and updating cues from context and prior outputs to probe stored experience. Such dynamic search underpins adaptive behavior, but theories still tackle directed recall task-by-task, tuning assumptions for each paradigm rather than modeling how goals steer retrieval across conditions. That fragmentation leaves us without a principled explanation for why identical study experiences can yield different recall patterns when goals shift, or how experience can sharpen or undercut control over search across time.

_This project aims to develop and test a computational model of human memory search that mechanistically explains how retrieval goals and experience jointly determine recall behavior and electrophysiological brain activity across directed retrieval tasks._ The model challenges a longstanding assumption in memory research: That accessibility can be measured and modeled separately from intentional control and retrieval strategy (Murdock, 1962; Kahana, 1996; Polyn, et. al., 2009; Hintzman, 2011; Cohen & Kahana, 2022). In my framework, explicit goal states configure memory search during both encoding and retrieval: Traces whose features decode as goal-congruent receive prioritized processing at study and accumulate evidence faster during recall, yielding concrete predictions for recall order, latency, and off-goal outputs. Integrating the controller into established memory models lets the system handle different instructions, timing manipulations, and practice histories without retooling the underlying architecture for each task. This unified mechanism explains when control falters (producing intrusions) and how neural markers such as late positive potentials and encoding–retrieval similarity track shifts in goal alignment. In turn, the model lays the groundwork for a comprehensive account of directed remembering that spans laboratory tasks and clarifies how goal-directed memory search succeeds or fails in both healthy and clinical populations.

_Our approach is to collect behavioral recall data and electrophysiological recordings while participants perform directed memory-search tasks that vary retrieval instruction, instruction timing, and matched item types, allowing us to iteratively refine the computational model._ The model’s core builds on retrieved-context frameworks (Howard & Kahana, 2002; Polyn et al., 2009) in which a gradually changing temporal context is bound to each episodic trace and later serves as the cue for recall. This temporal-targeting mechanism already explains benchmark phenomena such as lag-recency curves, latency distributions, and EEG signatures tied to context reinstatement, yet lacks an explicit mechanism for how goals shape the competition among traces. CMR2 (Lohnas et. al., 2015) introduced a context-monitoring gate that suppresses prior-list intrusions by rejecting candidates after selection, but it does not address goal-conditioned control across other instructions. We offer an alternative by embedding a goal-state controller that modulates processing resources and accumulation dynamics so goal-congruent traces gain priority across instructions, timing conditions, and practice histories. The proposed experiments stress-test the framework by comparing goal-control variants that posit different ways of allocating processing resources and enforcing retrieval policies, identifying behavioral and EEG constraints to arbitrate among them. We will deploy this integrated approach to accomplish the following specific aims:

Aim 1: Formalize goal-conditioned control within retrieved-context memory search. We will extend the base model with explicit goal states that modulate processing during encoding and retrieval, then fit it to existing datasets pairing performance on distinct directed-recall tasks including free-recall, serial-recall, categorized, and reward-prioritized recall. Simulations will test whether goal-congruent traces need accelerated accumulation (value-sensitive race) or context monitoring to capture instruction-dependent shifts in order, latency, and intrusion patterns.

Aim 2: Experimentally dissociate goal effects at encoding versus retrieval. In new directed-recall experiments we will manipulate retrieval instruction (free, serial, reward-maximizing, categorized), instruction timing (pre- vs post-study), and practice history while recording behavior plus EEG (late positive potentials at study, encoding–retrieval similarity at test). These data will adjudicate among the Aim 1 controllers by showing how goal information delivered before or after encoding reallocates attention, reorders recall trajectories, and shapes neural signatures of goal alignment.

Aim 3: Test how item value and affect interact with goal-configured control. Augment the Aim 2 paradigms by embedding reward-tagged and emotional items at matched list positions and compare the goal-controlled model against a variant that includes an additional context signal representing item value/affect (as proposed in emotional retrieved-context accounts; Talmi et. al., 2018). Behavioral outputs and EEG markers will show whether high-arousal or high-value items simply act like bottom-up goal signals or require this separate context-tagging mechanism, clarifying how emotional memories align with or override intentional retrieval control in both healthy and clinically relevant settings.

Memory search

The lines of research investigating working memory (i.e., short-term memory) and long-term memory have diverged during the last several decades, such that the relationship between working memory and long-term memory storage is rarely discussed and theoretical treatments of these different types of memory storage often do not acknowledge the role played by the other memory system (Cowan 2016). Although this is a simplifying assumption of much memory research, this narrowing of theoretical focus greatly limits our understanding of human memory generally, preventing us from constructing unified models of the human memory system.

The goal of this project is to develop and test a computational model of human memory that provides mechanistic explanations for human performance and electrophysiological brain activity in visual memory tasks using cognitive machinery developed to explain performance and brain activity in long-term memory tasks. This model challenges a foundational proposal in both psychology and neuroscience: That information must be temporarily held in short-term memory in order for long-term memory traces to be constructed (Hebb 1949, James 1890, Shiffrin & Atkinson 1969). In this modeling framework, long-term memories begin to be constructed as soon as an item is attended and working memory processes reactivate and manipulate these long-term memories to support flexible human behavior. By reaching across the theoretical divide between working and long-term memory research, we hope to bring the divergent lines of work examining working memory and long-term memory together. This theoretical unification will lay the foundation for a comprehensive model of human memory that will provide new insights into the continuity of working memory and long-term memory deficits in memory disorders and cognitive impairments in aging.

To develop our model, which we call CMR3, we used a list-learning paradigm. The list-learning paradigm is especially useful for modeling intrusions due to its ability to capture two apparently distinct processes that both share the episodic memory system: voluntary memory (strategic, in response to a targeted memory search) and involuntary memory (spontaneous and unintended). Some theorists have proposed that each type of memory should take place through distinct mechanisms; however, it is increasingly understood that both voluntary (strategic) and involuntary autobiographical memories operate through the same episodic memory system (Berntsen, 2010). Specifically, whereas people may generate additional mental context cues to guide the intended retrieval of memories, their voluntary recall is still determined by their current context. Autobiographical memories that may be experienced as “spontaneous” are also reactivated by the same process of encountering an associated spatial, temporal, emotional or other related contextual cue. Accordingly, when patients with PTSD report experiencing apparently-spontaneous memory intrusions, typically further investigation reveals the presence of one or more associated temporal, spatial, perceptual or other context cues associated to the trauma that cued the memory (Ehlers & Clark, 2000).

The externalized free recall paradigm is well suited for modeling intrusive involuntary recall because it operationalizes how both voluntary (strategic) and involuntary (spontaneous) recall outputs occur when the episodic memory system is engaged. During an externalized free recall task, subjects are instructed to report all words that enter their mind, regardless of whether or not they are trying to recall the words. In addition to the recall outputs that they report during a typical delayed free recall task, here subjects also report all other spontaneous recalls that occur during retrieval, even when subjects are aware that this spontaneous background noise is not from the intended target context for recall. Thus, intrusions are actually the result of a spontaneous, unintended process that arises as a byproduct during the subject’s intentional retrieval search. In this paradigm, intrusions occur as an unintended and undesired process that subjects must try to censor as they maintain their focus on their desired mental content (i.e., the correct results of the recall search)

> In free recall, a list of words is presented and the subject tries to recall as many as possible in any order. This task fit perfectly into the new revolutionary environment. In free recall, subjects were permitted and even encouraged to ‘‘do their own thing,’’ which was given the more dignified name of subjective organization (Tulving, 1962). For a decade or so, memory researchers investigated how subjects imposed organization on word lists and devised ways to reduce this organization to its own ‘‘quantifying statistical analysis’’ (e.g., Frankel & Cole, 1971). Other researchers were intrigued by the recall advantage displayed by items near the beginning and end of the list and used such primacy and recency effects to infer the fixed architecture of the memory system—most prominently, the classical distinction between short-term and long-term memory stores. I attribute the downfall of free recall to the gradual and largely tacit realization that such efforts are inconsistent with the fact that free-recall subjects do their own thing. Allowing subjects to recall the words in any order they wish encourages them to explore various encoding and retrieval strategies. In a multilist free-recall experiment with a constant list length, subjects learn to process words near the end of the list differently from earlier items (e.g., Craik, 1970; Watkins & Watkins, 1974). Recent evidence also suggests that subjects employ different strategies for lists of low-frequency, high-frequency, and mixed-frequency words (McDaniel & Bugg, 2008). The few researchers who dare to ask their subjects about the strategies they used find that the reports correlate with performance. Some subjects use ‘‘shallow’’ rehearsal strategies throughout a study list, and others use semantic strategies such as making up a story. Not surprisingly, semantic strategies support better levels of recall. And in a multilist design, the same subject may use strategies that vary from one list to the next. Over successive lists, according to Delany and Knowles (2004, p. 121), ‘‘ ... there is evidence that practice may lead participants to abandon shallow encoding strategies like rote rehearsal in favor of more elaborative strategies like making up a story ... .’’ It seems obvious that the subject’s study strategy will affect order of recall, and that recall order will interact with the primacy and recency portions of the list (Dalezman, 1976). This creates a nightmare for the theorist who wants to understand what really is going on. According to Haberlandt (1999, pp. 25–26), a discussion of different strategies and their effects on free recall was published in the 19th century (Binet & Henri, 1894). A great waste of effort might have been prevented if that article had been translated into English. In the analysis of any cognitive task, one should avoid averaging over strategies because the processes either are different or are deployed in different ways. This danger is particularly acute in free recall, where just about any strategy is likely to interact with the two ends of the list and contribute to primacy and recency effects. Despite this problem, one still sees scattered attempts to model free recall in terms of fixed memory architecture (e.g., Polyn, Norman, & Kahana, 2009). To my knowledge, even the best of such efforts does not explain how subjects keep track of which items they have recalled, so they can avoid producing them again (e.g., Gardiner & Klee, 1976). This in itself is a memory question that would have to be dealt with by an adequate theory of free recall. Free recall experiments are easy to run, and the resulting data can be analyzed in a multitude of ways, posing an intellectual challenge for a modeler. But the overlay of study and retrieval strategies makes the task a grotesque, neither-fishnor-fowl creature of the laboratory—corresponding to nothing people do in everyday life and too complex to be of much use for scientific analysis. It is no wonder that its popularity has declined.
