Retrieved-context theory proposes that context evolves by reinstating item-dependent features.
This predicts associative interference when retrieving lists containing item repetitions:
contexts that are good cues for one position tend to be good cues for other positions.

Hmm, but this is only one part of the equation.
The other part is the composite architecutre of CMR.
Or specifically the notion that items are associated with a composite of contexts associated with the item.
This is still the idea that context evolves by reinstating item-dependent features.

Both problems stem from the treatment of the _item_ as the primary unit of context-based retrieval.
At encoding and retrieval, the mistake is driving context evolution by item->context associations instead of...something else.

The beffudling thing is the positive thesis that replaces item-driven reinstatement.
In retrieval, there's a clear thing...a specific trace, a single study event.
