This document records our progress and key decisions as we build a brain-based version of eCMR using EEG data from Zarubin et al. (2020).

Prior analyses provide two anchors. First, emotionally negative items are recalled more often than neutral items. Second, late positive potential (LPP) amplitudes during study predict later recall for emotional items but not neutral items. These patterns relate brain signals and emotion to performance but do not specify mechanisms that generate recall.

We address this with computational neurocognitive models that formalize hypotheses about how LPPs interact with attention and context binding at encoding. We simulate trials and score models by the likelihood they assign to the full recalled set on each list, asking how probable the observed data are under each mechanism as specified. Good fits provide evidence that a mechanism can produce the behavior; poor fits indicate the specification is insufficient.

Our evaluation instantiates these hypotheses within the retrieved-context framework of eCMR (Talmi et al., 2019), where item–context associations guide recall. Emotional items bind more strongly to emotion-specific context and attract more attention, increasing their odds of retrieval. We compare ways LPPs modulate these processes and assess which specification renders the observed recalled sets most probable, moving beyond correlation toward mechanism.

1 Data Preparation

The primary analysis table is Single_Trial_Behavioural_and_EEG_Data_Z.csv (Z:\Talmi lab space\Archive\Secondary_Data_Analysis_Zarubin_From_Robin\Manuscript\files from robin nov24\). That table, identical across server copies, omits study events that lack reliable EEG measurements. To restore those events, we merged it with the behavioral log All_Included_Subjects.csv (Z:\Talmi lab space\Archive\Secondary_Data_Analysis_Zarubin_From_Robin\Data\Behaviour\Behaviour_csv_files\). This log contains no LPP scores but records recall status for all study events beyond a primacy buffer of the first two items per list. Recall data for buffer items were excluded by the original authors to avoid confounding primacy effects with emotion–memory effects, and could be recovered.

Before merging, we verified label consistency across sources, confirming there were no conflicts in recalled versus not-recalled designations. The merged dataset comprises 6,840 study events from 342 list trials with 20 analyzable items per list. Of these events, 371 (5.42%) lack EEG measurements. Missingness is comparable across valence conditions: negative lists contribute 125 of 2,266 events (5.52%; mean 0.37 ± 0.65 items missing per trial) and neutral lists contribute 246 of 4,574 events (5.38%; mean 0.72 ± 0.93 items missing per trial). Overall, 202 of 342 lists (59.1%) include at least one missing LPP value; among affected lists, 1.84 ± 1.01 items are missing (median 2; maximum 6). Across participants, missing exposure ranges from 2 to 19 items (3.9–10% of each person’s 180 study events), so imputation methodologically substantive.

LPP amplitudes are imputed using within-person, within-trial emotion-matched means. For any study event still missing an LPP value after merging, we fill that participant’s average for items of the same emotion within the same trial. This preserves within‑person, within‑trial, and emotion‑specific structure and avoids borrowing information across trials or valence conditions.

The dataset indicates which items were recalled but not their order. Accordingly, model fitting uses loss functions defined over the recalled set per trial, and our likelihood-based evaluation scores models by the probability they assign to the observed recalled sets rather than to output sequences. Because primacy buffers cannot be recovered, lists are analyzed as 20‑item sequences, despite originally being 22 items long.

2 Benchmark Analyses

These benchmarks both define concrete targets for model development and verify that data preparation preserved known effects. Unless noted, error bars show 95% bootstrapped confidence intervals across participants.

2.1 Emotional Enhancement of Memory

The central behavioral pattern is the emotional enhancement of memory (EEM): emotionally negative items are recalled more often than neutral items. We benchmark EEM with a categorized serial position curve (Category-SPC): for each study position (1–20), we tabulate recall rates separately for emotional and neutral items. Emotional items are recalled more often across all study positions, replicating the reference analysis used in the Daw meeting slides and confirming our data pipeline. Effect summary: mean recall difference (emotional – neutral), averaged across positions = 0.125, 95% CI [0.090, 0.162]; positions with emotional > neutral = 20/20.

2.2 LPP Is Higher for Emotional Items

We examine LPP amplitudes by study position and item type, separately for Early LPP (400–700 ms) and Late LPP (700–1,000 ms). In both windows, mean LPPs are higher for emotional items than for neutral items across positions. However, variability is large and position-wise means fluctuate, so distributions overlap substantially; this motivates treating LPP and item type as distinct signals. Effect summary: mean Early-LPP difference (emotional − neutral) across positions = 0.405 µV, 95% CI [0.280, 0.516]; Late-LPP difference = 0.354 µV, 95% CI [0.219, 0.484]; standardized difference (Cohen’s d_Early, d_Late) = 0.23, 0.18.

2.3 LPP Predicts Recall More Strongly for Emotional Items

To relate LPP to behavior, we plot LPP by study position and recall status, drawing separate plots by item type and LPP window Figure 3. The clearest separation is in the Early window for emotional items: recalled emotional items show higher Early LPPs than unrecalled emotional items. For neutral items, the recalled vs unrecalled difference is small. Thus, Early LPP relates to recall in both types but more strongly for emotional items. Effect summary: Early-LPP difference (recalled − unrecalled) for emotional items = 0.43 µV, 95% CI [0.27, 0.58]; for neutral items = 0.10 µV, 95% CI [−0.07, 0.28]. Late-window differences (emotional, neutral) = 0.20 µV [0.04, 0.38], 0.03 µV [−0.14, 0.20].

In Figure 4, we also superimpose Early-LPP curves to highlight joint structure: recalled emotional items have the highest Early LPPs. Unrecalled emotional items overlap with both recalled and unrecalled neutral items. If LPP simply encoded item type, curves for recalled and unrecalled items of the same type would align. If it encoded recall probability alone, curves for recalled items across types would align. Neither pattern holds, indicating an interation between LPP and item type.

Finally, we summarize Early-LPP contrasts with mean differences and 95% confidence intervals in Figure 5. The pairwise contrasts underscore that Early LPP peaks only when both emotion and later recall line up. Recalled negatives sit significantly above every other group, and even unrecalled negatives stay elevated relative to unrecalled neutrals, so emotional encoding leaves a residual boost. But the key null result is that unrecalled negatives are statistically indistinguishable from recalled neutrals: once we hold recall constant, the emotion tag alone no longer guarantees higher LPP. Likewise, neutral recalled vs. unrecalled items overlap. Together these patterns show that Early LPP is neither a pure emotion marker nor a pure recall marker. It primarily signals the conjunction of the two!

2.3.1 Statistical Cross-Check

Prior work using generalized mixed-effects models provides an alternative perspective on these relationships. Figure 6 predicts recall as a linear function of condition (emotional vs. neutral), Early LPP, and their interaction. It shows significant main effects of both factors and a significant interaction. This aligns with the plots: a baseline recall advantage for emotional items, an additional boost from early LPP, and a stronger LPP effect for emotional items than for neutral items.

2.4 Recall Sets Reflect a Temporal Contiguity Effect

Classic work on free recall shows that successive recalls tend to come from nearby study positions, a temporal contiguity effect. Although recall order is not available here, we can still ask whether recalled sets show an analogous structure: are items studied close together more likely to be recalled together than items studied far apart? To quantify this, we compute an order-agnostic co-recall curve. For each list and each unordered pair of study positions separated by lag \(d = |j - i|\), we estimate the probability that both items are recalled, \(\text{CoRec}(d)\), as the proportion of such pairs where both positions were marked as recalled. This yields, for lags 1–19, a temporal contiguity curve describing how often pairs of items separated by a given study lag co-occur in the recalled set.

As shown in Figure 7, co-recall probabilities are highest for short lags and decline monotonically with increasing separation, indicating that recall sets are temporally clustered even when we ignore output order. P airs of items studied one or two positions apart are much more likely to be recalled together than pairs separated by eight or more positions. Effect summary: mean co-recall for lags 1–3 is [X₁], 95% CI [L₁, U₁], compared with [X₂], 95% CI [L₂, U₂], for lags 8–10; the difference (=) [X₁ − X₂], 95% CI [LΔ, UΔ], confirms a robust temporal contiguity effect at the level of recalled sets. This benchmark sets a clear target for model development: candidate mechanisms must not only reproduce itemwise recall probabilities but also the tendency for nearby items in the study list to be recalled together.

2.5 Implications for Model Development

Benchmarks imply that item type and LPP should be treated as distinct inputs whose effects interact. Item type confers a baseline recall advantage (EEM), and early LPP further modulates recall likelihood, more strongly for emotional items. Mechanistic models should therefore allow LPP to influence attention and/or context binding in a way that depends on item type. In turn, they can be evaluated by the probability they assign to the full recalled set on each list. Our GMM cross-check supports this interpretation and specifically confirm significant linear relationships between predictors, recall, and their interaction. To keep initial model development tractable, we can similarly assume linearity in how LPP interacts with item type to modulate attention and/or context binding.

Figure 3 rules out two attractive accounts of how LPP relates to item type and recall. The first is that LPP reflects item type: emotional items have higher LPPs, and this drives their recall advantage. We do observe higher average LPPs for emotional items, but distributions overlap substantially. Furthermore, if LPP simply encoded item type, recalled and unrecalled items of the same type would show aligned LPPs. This does not hold: LPP curves split by recall status diverge within item types, even to the extent that LPPs for unrecalled emotional items largely overlap with those for both recalled and unrecalled neutral items.

The second attractive account is that LPP reflects recall probability: items with higher LPPs are more likely to be recalled, regardless of item type. If this were true, recalled items across item types would show aligned LPPs. This also does not hold: LPP curves split by item type diverge within recall status, with recalled emotional items showing the highest LPPs. Instead, LPP appears to signal the conjunction of item type and recall status, predicting recall more strongly for emotional items than for neutral items. Our models should therefore incorporate both item type and LPP signals as distinct, interacting influences on recall in order to avoid mispredicting either unrecalled emotional items or recalled neutral items.

3.2 Recall Rate by Early LPP

This analysis is deprecated. It binned items by LPP amplitude within item category and plotted recall rates per bin to check whether LPP predicts recall beyond the emotional/neutral label. Bins were defined by percentiles of the pooled LPP distribution, and results were shown separately for Early LPP (400–700 ms) and Late LPP (700–1,000 ms). LPP values were the provided z‑normalized amplitudes computed per electrode and trial by subtracting the −200–0 ms baseline and dividing by the baseline SD (so values are on a mean‑0, SD‑1 scale). An earlier plotting bug set a participant’s bin value to zero when they contributed no items to that bin, which biased sparse bins downward; this has been fixed by excluding empty participant–bin cells from the proportion and confidence‑interval calculations. The reference figure displays lines predicted by a generalized mixed‑effects model of recall as a function of LPP and item category, whereas the current plot shows empirical proportions within bins; the two approaches diverge most at distribution tails where data are sparse.

As seen in Figure 9, recall rates rise with Early LPP for emotional items and are comparatively flatter for neutral items. Observations were distributed unevenly across bins (see count panels in Figure 10). Because there are more neutral than emotional items overall, neutral items appear more frequently in every LPP bin. However, since recall rates are proportions within bin, this does not bias the curves. Still, wide intervals at extreme LPP values reflect very low occupancy in those bins (and thus high uncertainty about recall rates there).

We deprecate this analysis because binning introduces instability and comparability problems: tail bins above roughly 4.86 and below −3.25 contain few observations, global percentile bins balance totals but not per‑category counts, and bin‑edge choices can change the apparent trend without changing the underlying data. Rather than redefine or stabilize the binning procedure, we opt to retire it. For inference and model development we instead rely on unbinned methods (generalized mixed‑effects models and likelihood‑based evaluation of full recalled sets) and on visualizations that condition on recall status without binning (e.g., Figure 3). The plots are retained only as qualitative checks against the reference analysis.
